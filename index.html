Methodology

Overview: This engine uses a Monte Carlo simulation approach to model the 2026 NCAA Tournament. Each game is simulated by calculating an expected point margin between two teams, then adding gaussian random noise to represent real game-to-game variance. The bracket is simulated thousands of times to produce probability distributions for each team's advancement.

1. Core Efficiency Model

The backbone of every prediction is adjusted efficiency margin. For each matchup, we estimate points per possession for each team using the formula:

Team A PPP = (A's AdjOE × B's AdjDE) / National Avg Efficiency

The expected margin is then (A_PPP − B_PPP) × avg_possessions / 100. This is the standard tempo-free approach used by KenPom, Barttorvik, and most serious college basketball models.

2. Data Sources

All team statistics are sourced from Barttorvik (barttorvik.com), a free and widely-respected college basketball analytics site.

AdjOE, AdjDE, TempoFull-season adjusted efficiency ratings from 2026_team_results.csv (T-Rank data)
SOSStrength of schedule from the same CSV (column 16), Barttorvik's composite schedule strength metric
MomentumDerived from Barttorvik's H-Rank page (last 10 games adjusted efficiency). Calculated as the delta between last-10 efficiency margin and full-season efficiency margin, normalized to 0–1. Above 0.5 = peaking, below 0.5 = fading.
ExperienceComposite score derived from three factors: (1) tournament appearances in the last 5 years (2022-2026), weighted 35%; (2) deepest recent tournament run (championship=1.0, Final Four=0.8, Elite Eight=0.65, Sweet 16=0.5, etc.), weighted 35%; (3) program pedigree tier (blue blood=1.0, elite program=0.67, solid program=0.33, mid/low-major=0.0), weighted 30%. Sources: NCAA.com tournament history, ESPN Champ Week tracker, Wikipedia tournament bids by school.

3. Game Simulation

Each simulated game follows this process:

a) Calculate the expected margin = (efficiency component × efficiency weight) + (SOS delta × SOS weight) + (momentum delta × momentum weight) + (experience delta × experience weight)

b) Generate a random game outcome by adding gaussian noise: actual_margin = expected_margin + N(0, σ) where σ is controlled by the Game Variance slider. At variance=1.0, σ ≈ 11 points, matching real college basketball game-to-game standard deviation.

c) If the simulated margin is positive, Team A wins. If negative, Team B wins. This is how upsets naturally emerge — a team expected to win by 5 points has a meaningful chance of losing when the noise swings -8 or more.

4. Monte Carlo Method

The "Run Monte Carlo" button simulates the entire 67-game tournament thousands of times (configurable from 1K to 1M). Each simulation plays every game from the Round of 64 through the Championship. Results are aggregated to produce probability distributions — the percentage of simulations in which each team reaches each round.

5. Bracket Selection (Pick One Bracket)

The "Pick One Bracket" feature uses rejection sampling to find a historically reasonable bracket. It simulates brackets in a loop and counts first-round upsets (lower seed beats higher seed). Brackets are accepted only if they fall within historical norms: 4–10 first-round upsets (historical average ~6.2) and 6–16 total upsets (historical average ~12–13). This filters out unreasonably chalky or chaotic brackets without distorting the model's actual predictions. Approximately 90% of raw simulations pass this filter on the first attempt.

6. Weight Descriptions

Efficiency (0–2)Scales the raw efficiency margin. At 1.0, used as-is. At 0, efficiency is entirely ignored (all teams are equal before contextual factors). At 2.0, efficiency gaps are doubled.
SOS (0–5)Points added/subtracted per unit of strength-of-schedule difference. Penalizes teams with weak schedules (mid-majors) and rewards battle-tested teams.
Momentum (0–5)Points added/subtracted per unit of momentum difference. Data-driven from Barttorvik H-Rank (last 10 games efficiency vs season average). Captures teams peaking or fading entering the tournament.
Experience (0–5)Points added/subtracted per unit of experience difference. Data-driven composite of tournament frequency (last 5 years), deepest recent run, and program pedigree. Kansas/Houston (0.97) vs Cal Baptist (0.07) = 0.9 unit gap × weight.
Variance (0–5)Controls game-to-game randomness (σ). At 0: σ=4pts (nearly deterministic). At 1.0: σ=11pts (matches real basketball). At 2+: increased chaos. This is the primary lever for upset frequency.

7. Limitations & Future Work

Roster-level experience is not captured. The experience factor measures program-level tournament history, not whether specific players on this year's roster have March minutes. A team that returned all five starters from a Final Four run has a different edge than a team of transfers arriving at a historically strong program.

Injuries are not modeled. Gonzaga's Braden Huff injury, North Carolina losing Caleb Wilson, and Duke's Caleb Foster absence all materially affect outcomes but aren't reflected in the efficiency data unless they happened early enough to show up in the season averages.

Matchup-specific factors like style of play interactions (e.g., a slow-paced defensive team may neutralize a fast-paced offense more than the raw numbers suggest) are not captured.

Barttorvik data columns not yet incorporated: Qual O/Qual D (quality-game-only efficiency), Elite SOS, WAB (Wins Above Bubble), and conference-only efficiency are all available in the source data and could add additional predictive layers.
